Covariance in a single dimension

In this and the following posts, we’ll return repeatedly to the case of a univariate random variable, in which case the covariance reduces to the ordinary variance. This will let us test out computations in a simple setting, and will also provide good intuition. In particular, many of the differentio-geometric properties of the covariance have simple interpretations in the one-dimensional case, which are helpful for building intuition for some of the concepts we’ll be discussing without digging too deep into the mathematical concepts themselves.

For now, we’ll be a bit loose and, for example, ignore the distinction between the variance of a sample and a distribution. We recall the sample variance \[ s^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i (x_i - \bar{x}) \] where \(n\) is the sample size and \(\bar{x}\) is the sample mean. We’re not going to bother with whether we divide by \(n\) or \(n-1\) – the only thing we care about is the observation that the variance is the average squared distance from the mean, which provides good enough intuition for why the calculation gives us a measure of spread.

Note that it follows immediately that \(s^2\) must be greater than or equal to zero, and is zero exactly when all observations are equal to the mean (that is, when the sample is constant). This is a degenerate case, and we’ll almost always assume that the variance is positive, but keep this boundary case in mind. In fact, we’ll take this a little bit further and identify the variance with the non-negative real numbers \([0, \infty)\). Think of this as the “space” of variances.

Three views on the variance

We already know that the variance can be seen as a measure of the spread of a sample or distribution. Here are two other perspectives that will be useful to us:

Variance as a transformation

It is a basic property of the variance that for any random variable \(x\) with variance \(s^2\), and any real number \(a \geq 0\), then \(ax\) has variance \(a^2s^2\). In other words, variances scale quadratically. We can take a perspective on this that will be useful later: Every variance \(s^2\) has an associated value \(s\) (the square root, or the standard deviation in this case, but forget about that) that transforms (by multiplication) a random variable with variance \(1\) to have variance \(s^2\). As a particular useful example: Suppose we want to transform a random variable \(x\) with variance \(s_1^2\) to one with variance \(s_2^2\). First, we can “whiten” \(x\) by applying \((s_1^2)^{-\frac{1}{2}}x = \frac{1}{s_1}x\) to achieve a unit variance, and then apply \((s_2)(s_1^2)^{-\frac{1}{2}}x = \frac{s_2}{s_1}x\) to get a variance of \(s_2^2\).
